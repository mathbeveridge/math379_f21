Proposition 4.33 shows that when \(i\) and \(j\) are distinct elements of \([n]\text{,}\) then exactly half of all \(n\)-permutations contain \(i\) and \(j\) in the same cycle.

  1. Prove that when \(i\text{,}\) \(j\) and \(k\) are distinct elements of \([n]\text{,}\) then exactly one third of all \(n\)-permutations contain \(i\text{,}\) \(j\) and \(k\) in the same cycle.

  2. Generalize part (a). When \(i_1, i_2, \ldots , i_k\) are distinct elements of \([n]\text{,}\) what fraction of all \(n\)-permutations contain these elements in the same cycle? Prove your answer.
