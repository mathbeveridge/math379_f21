Let \(G_k(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} g_{n,k} \frac{x^n}{n!}\) and \(H_k(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} h_{n,k} \frac{x^n}{n!}\) be the exponential generating functions for these sequences. We have

\begin{equation*} G_1(x) = H_1(x) = \exp(x) \end{equation*}

since the identity permutation is the only \(1\)-volution. Example 4.36 on page 202 shows that

\begin{equation*} G_2(x) = \exp \left( x + \frac{x^2}{2} \right) \end{equation*}

and therefore

\begin{equation*} H_2(x) = \exp \left( x + \frac{x^2}{2} \right) - \exp\left( x \right) \end{equation*}

  1. Find \(G_3(x)\) and \(H_3(x)\text{.}\)

  2. Find \(G_4(x)\) and \(H_4(x)\text{.}\)

  3. Let \(k \geq 1\) and suppose that \(p^k\) is the identity. Explain why the order of \(p\) must divide \(k\text{.}\) Use this fact to find a formula for \(G_k(x)\text{.}\)

  4. Find a formula for \(H_{6}(x)\) in terms of \(G_{k}(x)\) where \(1 \leq k \leq 6\text{.}\)

  5. Find a formula for \(H_{30}(x)\) in terms of \(G_{k}(x)\) where \(1 \leq k \leq 30\text{.}\)
